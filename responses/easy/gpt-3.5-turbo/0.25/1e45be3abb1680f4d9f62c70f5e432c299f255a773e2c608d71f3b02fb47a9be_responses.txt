6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

In this equation, carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) are converted into glucose (C6H12O6) and oxygen (O2) in the presence of light energy.

The process of photosynthesis can be divided into two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (also known as the Calvin cycle).

1. Light-dependent reactions:
These reactions take place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts and require light energy to occur. The main steps of the light-dependent reactions are as follows:
- Light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll molecules in the thylakoid membranes, causing them to become excited.
- The excited electrons are passed along a series of electron transport chains, generating ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate) as energy carriers.
- Water molecules are split into oxygen, protons, and electrons. The oxygen is released as a byproduct, while the electrons are used to replace the ones lost by chlorophyll molecules during photosynthesis.

- Some of the G3P molecules are used to regenerate RuBisCO and continue the Calvin cycle, while others are used to synthesize glucose and other carbohydrates.
