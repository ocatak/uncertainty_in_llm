2. Swallowing: Once the food is chewed and moistened in the mouth, it is swallowed and travels down the esophagus to the stomach.

3. Stomach: In the stomach, food is mixed with gastric juices that contain enzymes and acid to further break down food molecules. This mixture is referred to as chyme. The stomach also contracts to physically break down food and to move it into the small intestine.

4. Small Intestine: The chyme enters the small intestine, where most of the digestion and absorption of nutrients takes place. Enzymes from the pancreas, bile from the liver, and enzymes from the intestinal lining help break down the food and absorb nutrients, vitamins, minerals, fats, and proteins.

5. Large Intestine: Once the beneficial nutrients are absorbed, what is left passes into the large intestine where water is absorbed and waste products are formed. Bacteria inside the colon break down any remaining undigested food and produce certain vitamins, like vitamin K.

6. Rectum and Anus: Finally, waste, now called feces, is stored in the rectum until it is released through the anus during defecation.

Throughout the digestive process, various hormones and nerve signals are at work to regulate the release of digestive enzymes, control intestinal movements, and prompt satiety. Overall, the stages of the human digestive system work collectively to efficiently digest food, absorb nutrients, and eliminate excess waste.
