1. Lift: The wings of an airplane are specially shaped to provide lift, which is the force that keeps the airplane in the air. The wings are designed with a curved upper surface and a flatter lower surface, which creates a pressure difference between the upper and lower surfaces. This pressure difference creates an upward force that pushes the airplane into the air.

2. Weight: Weight is the force of gravity acting on the airplane, pulling it towards the ground. In order to stay in the air, the lift generated by the wings must be greater than the weight of the airplane.

3. Thrust: Thrust is the forward force that is generated by the engines of the airplane. As the engines produce thrust, they push the airplane forward, causing it to move through the air.

4. Drag: Drag is the resistance that the airplane encounters as it moves through the air. Drag is caused by factors such as air friction and the shape of the airplane. In order to maintain forward motion and stay in the air, the thrust generated by the engines must be greater than the drag force pushing against the airplane. 

By effectively balancing these four forces, airplanes are able to generate lift, overcome gravity, and stay in the air.
