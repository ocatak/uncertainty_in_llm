Response 1: Cellular respiration is the process by which cells in the body obtain energy from organic compounds. This process is essential for the functioning of all living organisms. The process of cellular respiration can be summarized into three main stages: 

1. Glycolysis: This is the first stage of cellular respiration and occurs in the cytoplasm of the cell. In this phase, complex sugars such as glucose are broken down into smaller molecules such as pyruvate. This process also generates a small amount of ATP (adenosine triphosphate). 

2. Citric Acid Cycle (also known as the Krebs cycle): The pyruvate molecules produced in glycolysis are then converted into Acetyl CoA and enter the mitochondria. Within the mitochondria, the Acetyl CoA molecules undergo a series of chemical reactions that further break down the molecules and release carbon dioxide as a byproduct. The energy released from these reactions is used to produce more ATP molecules.

3. Oxidative Phosphorylation: The final stage of cellular respiration occurs in the inner membrane of the mitochondria. Here, electrons are passed through a series of protein complexes known as the electron transport chain. As the electrons move through the protein complexes, hydrogen ions are pumped across the membrane. This creates a concentration gradient of ions that drives the enzyme ATP synthase to produce a high amount of ATP. At the end of the electron transport chain, oxygen ultimately serves as the final electron acceptor to produce water.

Through these three stages of cellular respiration, cells are able to effectively generate ATP molecules that provide them with the necessary energy for metabolism, growth, and other cellular functions.
