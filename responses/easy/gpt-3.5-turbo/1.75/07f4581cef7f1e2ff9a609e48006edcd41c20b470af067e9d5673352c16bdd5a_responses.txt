Response 46: Cellular respiration is the process by which living organisms produce energy for their cells by breaking down glucose molecules from the food they ingest. It takes place in the mitochondria of cells and consists of several steps:

1. Glycolysis: Glucose molecules are broken down into pyruvate molecules. This process occurs in the cytoplasm of cells and produces a small amount of ATP and NADH molecules.

2. Pyruvate oxidation: The pyruvate molecules enter the mitochondria and are converted to acetyl-CoA, releasing carbon dioxide in the process. This step generates more NADH molecules.

3. Citric acid cycle (also known as the Krebs cycle): Acetyl-CoA reacts with oxaloacetic acid to form citric acid, which is then broken down in a series of reactions that release carbon dioxide and produce ATP, NADH, and FADH2 molecules.

4. Electron transport chain: The high-energy electrons from NADH and FADH2 molecules are passed through a series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane, releasing energy that is used to generate a large amount of ATP through a process called oxidative phosphorylation.

Overall, cellular respiration produces a total of 36-38 molecules of ATP for each glucose molecule that is oxidized, providing the energy necessary for cells to carry out their various biochemical processes. It is a highly efficient way for living organisms to produce energy and maintain their biological functions.
Response 48: Cellular respiration is the process through which cells obtain energy by breaking down glucose (sugar) molecules to generate adenosine triphosphate (ATP), which is the cellular equivalent of fuel.

The process of cellular respiration takes place in three main stages:

1. Glycolysis: This initial stage of cellular respiration involves the splitting of glucose molecules into two molecules of pyruvate, resulting in a small amount of ATP production. Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm of the cell.

2. Citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle): During this stage, the pyruvate molecules are transported into the mitochondria (the powerhouse of the cell) and undergo a series of chemical reactions, resulting in the production of more ATP and electron carriers such as NADH and FADH2.

3. Electron transport chain: The electron carriers NADH and FADH2 shuffle electrons across a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As hydrogen ions and electrons are transported across the membrane, a proton gradient is generated, which provides the energy to produce a large amount of ATP through a process known as oxidative phosphorylation.

Overall, through these three main stages of cellular respiration, cells are able to efficiently harvest the energy stored in glucose molecules and convert it into a form (ATP) that can be used for various cellular activities, such as metabolism, growth, and maintenance.
