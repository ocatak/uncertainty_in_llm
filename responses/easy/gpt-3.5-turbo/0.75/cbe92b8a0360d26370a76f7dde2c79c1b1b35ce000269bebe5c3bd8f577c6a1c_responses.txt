Response 1: The life cycle of a butterfly is comprised of four stages: egg, larva (caterpillar), pupa (chrysalis), and adult butterfly.

1. Egg: The life cycle of a butterfly begins when a female butterfly lays her eggs on a host plant. The eggs are usually laid on the underside of a leaf and are often tiny and round in shape. The eggs may hatch within a few days or weeks, depending on the species of butterfly.

2. Larva (Caterpillar): Once the egg hatches, a caterpillar emerges. The caterpillar's main goal is to eat and grow quickly. It will molt several times, shedding its skin as it grows. The caterpillar may have distinct markings or colors that help camouflage it from predators.

3. Pupa (Chrysalis): When the caterpillar has reached its full size, it will enter the pupa stage. The caterpillar attaches itself to a plant or other surface and forms a chrysalis around itself. Inside the chrysalis, the caterpillar undergoes a dramatic transformation, breaking down its body and reorganizing into a butterfly.

4. Adult Butterfly: After a period of time, usually a few weeks, the adult butterfly emerges from the chrysalis. At first, its wings are small and crumpled, but the butterfly pumps fluid into them to expand and strengthen them. Once its wings are fully developed, the butterfly can fly and begin the cycle again by mating and laying eggs.

The life cycle of a butterfly is a fascinating process of metamorphosis and transformation, showcasing the beauty and resilience of these colorful insects.
