1. Absorption of sunlight: The first step in photosynthesis is the absorption of sunlight by chlorophyll, a pigment found in the chloroplasts of plant cells. Chlorophyll absorbs light in the blue and red wavelengths, and reflects green light, which gives plants their green color.

2. Water uptake: Plants absorb water from the soil through their roots. Water molecules are split into hydrogen and oxygen atoms in a process called photolysis, which takes place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts. The oxygen atoms are released as a byproduct into the atmosphere.

3. Carbon dioxide uptake: Plants also absorb carbon dioxide from the air through small pores called stomata on their leaves. The carbon dioxide molecules enter the chloroplasts and are used in the Calvin cycle, the second stage of photosynthesis.

4. Light-dependent reactions: In the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts, the absorbed sunlight is used to convert water molecules into oxygen, protons, and electrons. The electrons are passed along a series of proteins in the electron transport chain, generating ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate) as energy carriers for the next stage of photosynthesis.

5. Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions): The ATP and NADPH produced in the light-dependent reactions are used to convert carbon dioxide into glucose in the stroma of the chloroplasts. This process involves a series of chemical reactions that use the energy from ATP and NADPH to build glucose molecules from carbon dioxide.

6. Glucose production: The glucose molecules produced in the Calvin cycle are stored in the plant cells or used as energy sources for growth and metabolism. Oxygen is also released as a byproduct of photosynthesis and is released into the atmosphere through the stomata.

Overall, photosynthesis is a crucial process that provides energy for plants and oxygen for the atmosphere. It is a vital part of the Earth's ecosystem and plays a key role in the carbon cycle.
