The light energy is then used to convert carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) into glucose (C6H12O6) and oxygen (O2). This takes place in two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (also known as the Calvin cycle).

In the light-dependent reactions, chlorophyll absorbs light energy and uses it to split water molecules into oxygen, protons, and electrons. These electrons are then passed along a series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane of the chloroplast to generate ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH molecules, which will be used later in the Calvin cycle. The oxygen produced in this stage is released into the atmosphere.

In the light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle), the ATP and NADPH molecules generated in the light-dependent reactions are used to fix carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. This fixation process involves a series of reactions focused on converting carbon dioxide into glucose. The cycle starts with the enzyme Rubisco combining carbon dioxide with RuBP (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate) to form an unstable 6-carbon compound that quickly breaks down into two 3-carbon molecules. These molecules are eventually rearranged to form glucose and regenerate RuBP to keep the cycle going.

In the end, the glucose molecules produced are used by the plant for energy and growth, while some are stored for future use. The overall reaction of photosynthesis can be summarized as: 

6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2 

This process is vital for life on Earth as it is the foundation of the food chain, providing energy for plants and other organisms. Additionally, photosynthesis helps to maintain Earth's oxygen levels, contributing to the balance of the atmosphere.
