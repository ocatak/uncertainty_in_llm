Response 1: The life cycle of a butterfly consists of four stages: 

1. Egg: The life cycle of a butterfly starts when a female butterfly lays an egg on the leaves of a host plant. The egg is very small and typically white or yellow in color. 

2. Larva (Caterpillar): Once the egg hatches, a larva emerges from it known as a caterpillar. The caterpillar's main goal is to eat and grow. It sheds its skin several times in a process called molting, in order to accommodate its increasing size. 

3. Pupa (Chrysalis): After the caterpillar has grown to its full size and is ready to transform into a butterfly, it undergoes a process called metamorphosis. It spins a silk pad and attaches itself to a leaf or branch, where it sheds its outer skin to reveal a hard shell called a chrysalis. Inside the chrysalis, the caterpillar's body is broken down and reformulated into that of a butterfly. 

4. Adult: After a period of time, the adult butterfly emerges from the chrysalis, with uniquely folded wings. Its body is soft and swollen, but it will soon fill out and harden as the butterfly pumps fluid into its wings. Once fully formed, the butterfly will begin to fly and search for nectar to feed on. Adult butterflies will mate and repeat the cycle by laying eggs on host plants. 

The entire life cycle of a butterfly can vary in length depending on the species, but it typically lasts from a few weeks to a few months.
