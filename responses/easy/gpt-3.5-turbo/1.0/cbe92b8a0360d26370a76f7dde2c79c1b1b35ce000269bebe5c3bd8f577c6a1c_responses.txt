Response 1: The life cycle of a butterfly consists of four stages: egg, larva (caterpillar), pupa (chrysalis), and adult butterfly.

1. Egg: The life cycle of a butterfly begins when a female butterfly lays her eggs on a host plant. The eggs are usually laid on the underside of leaves to protect them from predators. The eggs are small, round, and often have a ridged or bumpy texture.

2. Larva (Caterpillar): Once the egg hatches, a larva, or caterpillar, emerges. The caterpillar's main job is to eat and grow. As it eats, the caterpillar's skin will shed and be replaced multiple times in a process called molting. The caterpillar's body will also change shape and color as it grows. Once the caterpillar has reached its full size, it will enter the next stage of the life cycle.

3. Pupa (Chrysalis): The caterpillar will attach itself to a secure surface, suspend itself in the air, and form a protective casing around itself called a chrysalis. Inside the chrysalis, the caterpillar undergoes a process called metamorphosis, in which it completely transforms into a butterfly. This process can take anywhere from a few days to a few weeks, depending on the species of butterfly.

4. Adult Butterfly: Once the transformation is complete, the adult butterfly will emerge from the chrysalis. At first, the butterfly's wings will be soft and wrinkled, but they will gradually expand and harden as the butterfly pumps fluid through them. The adult butterfly will then fly off in search of food and a mate to continue the life cycle.

The adult butterfly will eventually lay eggs on a host plant, starting the cycle over again. On average, the entire life cycle of a butterfly can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months, depending on the species and environmental conditions.
