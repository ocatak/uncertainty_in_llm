Response 1: The life cycle of a butterfly is comprised of four stages: egg, larva (caterpillar), pupa (chrysalis), and adult butterfly.

1. Egg: The life cycle of a butterfly begins when a female butterfly lays eggs on a host plant. The eggs are typically small and round, and can vary in color and shape depending on the species of butterfly.

2. Larva (Caterpillar): Once the egg hatches, a larva or caterpillar emerges. The caterpillar's main goal is to eat and grow rapidly. It will molt several times as it grows, shedding its exoskeleton to accommodate its increasing size. The caterpillar will continue to feed on the host plant until it is ready to enter the next stage of its life cycle.

3. Pupa (Chrysalis): When the caterpillar has reached its full size, it will form a protective casing around itself called a chrysalis or pupa. Inside the chrysalis, the caterpillar undergoes a process called metamorphosis, during which its body tissues are broken down and reorganized to form the adult butterfly. This stage can last anywhere from a few days to several weeks, depending on the species.

4. Adult Butterfly: Once the metamorphosis is complete, the adult butterfly emerges from the chrysalis. At this stage, the butterfly's wings are soft and crumpled, but they will gradually expand and harden as the butterfly pumps fluid into them. The adult butterfly will then begin its search for a mate and start the cycle over by laying eggs on a host plant.

The entire life cycle of a butterfly can vary in length depending on the species, environmental conditions, and other factors. Some butterflies may complete their life cycle in as little as a few weeks, while others may take several months.
